Stars and their Death

Stars are celestial bodies primarily composed of hydrogen and helium, where nuclear fusion in their cores transforms lighter elements into heavier ones, releasing energy. This process involves the fusion of hydrogen nuclei to form helium, generating light and heat. The star's gravitational collapse is counteracted by outward thermal pressure resulting from nuclear fusion, maintaining stability. Throughout a star's lifecycle, nucleosynthesis occurs, creating heavier elements through the r-process (rapid neutron capture) and the s-process (slow neutron capture). Neutron capture involves the absorption of neutrons by atomic nuclei, contributing to the formation of elements beyond helium.

A star's fate at the end of its life depends on its initial mass. (1 Solar mass = Mass of the Sun)

Low Mass Stars (Less than 8 solar masses):
  • As these stars exhaust their nuclear fuel (hydrogen to helium fusion in their cores), they expand into red giants.

  • In the red giant phase, the outer layers are expelled into space, creating a planetary nebula.

  • The core, which is mostly carbon and oxygen, contracts and becomes a white dwarf—a dense, Earth-sized remnant. Over a very long time, it cools and fades, eventually becoming a cold, dark object known as a black dwarf.

Intermediate Mass Stars (8-18 solar masses):
  • These stars experience a supernova explosion at the end of their lives. A supernova is a stellar explosion, where a star suddenly increases in brightness and releases an immense amount of energy, outshining its entire host galaxy. It is the result of the rapid and catastrophic collapse of a massive star's core.

  • After the explosion, the core of the star may become a neutron star if the star's core is between about 1.4 and 3 solar masses. Except for black holes, neutron stars are the densest objects in the universe, composed mainly of neutrons. Pulsars are the most common type of neutron stars and they can spin upto 716 rotations per second, they also have very strong magnetic fields which funnel jets of particles out along the two magnetic poles. If they have a binary companion, like another neutron star or black hole, they may merge eventually and create a black hole or a more massive neutron star.

High Mass Stars (Over 18 solar masses):
  • These stars have intense nuclear fusion and evolve rapidly. They undergo multiple stages of burning, creating increasingly heavier elements until iron is formed. These stars can become black holes after a supernova.

  • The core, unable to support itself against gravity, collapses rapidly. This collapse triggers a supernova explosion, expelling outer layers into space and If the core mass exceeds the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff (TOV) limit of about 3 solar masses, gravity overwhelms all forces and a black hole is formed. These are the densest objects in the universe and not even light can escape their gravity.

The Cosmic Web



The cosmic web is like a huge structure in space that shows how stuff is spread out across the universe. It's made up of big networks connecting dark matter and galaxies, kind of like a giant web. These networks create a pattern that looks like a web, with galaxies and dark matter connected like dots on the strands. Imagine it as a big cosmic quilt where galaxies are stitched together by invisible threads. This web shows how things are arranged in space on the largest scale. Scientists study it to understand how everything in the universe is connected and shaped by gravity.

The Cosmic Web



Formation of the Cosmic Web:

The cosmic web forms as a result of fluctuations in the early universe's matter density. Dark matter, affected by gravity, aggregates into halos, while gas is drawn into these regions, forming galaxies. The interconnected filaments of dark matter and gas create the cosmic web structure, with voids in less dense areas. Over billions of years, gravity amplifies these structures, leading to the large-scale pattern observed in the universe. This process is essential for understanding the universe's vast, filamentary structure.

Composition of the Cosmic Web:

  • Dark matter is a mysterious, invisible substance that does not emit, absorb, or reflect light. It interacts gravitationally and is thought to make up about 27% of the total mass-energy content of the universe. Dark energy is what causes the universe to expand faster and faster as time goes on. Dark matter is a crucial component in the formation and structure of the cosmic web.

  • Galaxies and galaxy clusters are embedded within the filaments of the cosmic web. The filaments serve as channels along which matter flows towards denser regions, leading to the formation of galaxies and clusters.

  • The cosmic web also contains diffuse gas, primarily hydrogen, in the form of hot and ionized plasma. This gas is responsible for the formation of galaxies and other cosmic structures. As gravity acts on the denser regions of the cosmic web, gas condenses and collapses to form galaxies and galaxy clusters.

Structure of the Cosmic Web:

  • Voids are vast, mostly empty regions in the cosmic web where the matter density is significantly lower than average. Galaxies and galaxy clusters tend to avoid these voids, contributing to the interconnected structure of the cosmic web.

  • Filaments are long, thread-like structures that connect nodes or clusters of galaxies. These filaments can be composed of dark matter, gas, and galaxies, creating a cosmic "skeleton" that binds the universe.

  • Nodes are the densest regions in the cosmic web, often located at the intersections of filaments. Galaxy clusters, which contain numerous galaxies, hot gas, and dark matter, form at these nodes due to the gravitational attraction of surrounding matter.



Spacetime

Spacetime is a fundamental concept in the framework of Einstein's theory of General Relativity. Spacetime fabric is a 4 dimensional plane, 3 dimensions of space and 1 dimension of time. In classical physics, space and time were treated as separate entities. However, in General Relativity, Einstein proposed that these two aspects are interconnected, forming a unified spacetime.

Gravity explained using spacetime:

Massive objects like planets or stars create a "dip" in the spacetime fabric, like placing a heavy ball on a trampoline. Now, if you place a smaller object, like a marble, near the massive one, it will roll towards the larger object. This movement isn't a force acting at a distance (as in Newtonian gravity), but rather the smaller object following the curvature created by the massive object in the spacetime fabric. Gravitational Waves:

Gravitational waves are ripples in spacetime caused by certain types of accelerating masses. According to Einstein's theory of general relativity, massive objects, like merging black holes or neutron stars, create waves in the fabric of spacetime as they move. These waves propagate outward, carrying energy across the universe. Gravitational wave detectors on Earth, like LIGO and Virgo, can measure tiny changes in distance caused by these waves passing through, allowing scientists to observe and study extreme cosmic events that emit gravitational waves.
